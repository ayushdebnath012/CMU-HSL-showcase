3D Gaussian Splatting: Scene Composition

I composited a separately-trained NeRF-Synthetic chair into the Mip-NeRF 360 Bicycle scene at the Gaussian-splat level — not 2D image compositing. I render the merged splat cloud through the original 3DGS rasterizer, so depth ordering and alpha blending between my asset and the scene resolve in a single pass.

Method of Composition

pscene = s · R · passet + t

This is the transform I apply to every foreground Gaussian:

Center
I map each Gaussian's mean through the similarity transform s·R·p + t.
Scale
I add + log(s) to the per-axis log-scales, so anisotropic extents scale consistently.
Rotation
I left-multiply each covariance quaternion by the placement rotation, q' = q_R ⊗ q.
Opacity
I edit this in alpha (logit) space, never on the raw sigmoid output.
Merge
I append the transformed foreground Gaussians to the scene Gaussians — one point cloud.
Render
I rasterize the merged cloud with the original Bicycle cameras; occlusion falls out for free.

Field Notes — Debugging Log

E-01
First composite came out barely visible. Turned out the runner script had its own scale/opacity defaults that were silently overriding what I'd set in the config, with much fainter values. Took a bit to notice — fixed by lining the defaults up with the config instead of fighting it.
E-02
Next render, the chair was just floating in mid-air. Rather than hand-picking another offset and hoping, I wrote ground_offset.py to work out the grounded translation from the scene's actual point cloud and camera calibration.
E-03
This was the ugly one — the chair rendered as a confetti-colored blob wrapped in a white halo. I assumed un-rotated SH coefficients at first, then a missing white-background training flag. Went and actually read the code for both before believing either — neither was it. Real cause: the chair had inherited the bicycle's VRAM-throttled densification schedule and never densified properly.
E-04
Retrained the chair with its own schedule and the fog got better but didn't fully go away. Two things left: genuine floater Gaussians (four filters — opacity, scale, spatial core-crop, color/opacity signature — cleared most of them) and view-dependent SH color baked in from the white background, which I zeroed out.
E-05
This one I missed and had to be told about: the chair had been lying on its back the entire time. Every rotation test up to that point only checked yaw, never pitch. NeRF-Synthetic chairs are Blender-style, local +Z up — but this scene's real up axis sits close to −Y. A hand-picked 90° pitch fix stood it upright and roughly doubled how much of the orbit actually looked usable.
E-06
That 90° number was a guess that happened to work, not a real answer. Swapped it for an exact Rodrigues rotation that aligns the chair's local up axis to the scene's actual measured up vector — which turned out to be tilted about 13°, not a clean cardinal axis. Round-tripped 2,000+ random angles through the Euler decomposition in a unit test before trusting it on the real data.
E-07
Added appearance matching so the chair's color and opacity drift toward the local scene average (blend = 0.35) instead of sitting there looking pasted-on. Kept a hard floor on the opacity multiplier so this can never quietly bring back the invisibility problem from step one.
E-08
Ran a third full 120-frame orbit sweep after all of the above. Checked every frame — zero of them show the old unrecognizable-ghost failure anymore.

Known Limitation — Source-Asset Artifact

From roughly a third of viewing angles, the chair's own NeRF-Synthetic reconstruction shows a translucent “ghost” artifact with speckle around the legs and base. I isolated it with a standalone render, a yaw sweep, and repeated orbit sweeps — it's a property of the chair's own training, a weak multi-view constraint from single-object, blank-background NeRF-Synthetic data, not a compositing or placement bug on my end. I couldn't fix it with any more placement or pruning changes; that would mean going back and addressing the chair's training itself.

Novel Extension

Shipped

One-shot statistical appearance matching. I blend the asset's color and opacity toward the local scene statistics around the placement point (blend = 0.35), with a hard opacity floor so the asset always stays visible. A single global correction — cheap, deterministic, and it's already in my pipeline.

Proposed Next Step

A learned correction. What I'd try next: keep the geometry fixed, render my composed model from several real Bicycle cameras, and optimize a low-dimensional color/exposure correction for the asset's SH coefficients directly against that rendered feedback — accounting for view-dependent lighting instead of one global blend.
